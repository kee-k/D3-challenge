Slight Correlation Discovered between Rates of Obesity and Poverty

An interesting discovery indeed - 2014 Census data from the United States shows a slight correlation between rates of obesity and poverty. Being a country that has been known to be affected by high rates of obesity, due to an abundance of fast food and poor levels of nutritional awareness, it is also interesting to consider how poverty might play into this. This is because in most cases, junk food and fast food is more affordable than healthier options. For example, cola is cheaper than water, and a meal at McDonalds is cheaper than salad ingredients. Higher rates of poverty might also indicate lower access to quality education regarding the importance of nutritional balance, which may again result in higher rates of obesity. Although there is much more to be taken into consideration here, the graphic above does give an initial insight into the relationship between obesity and poverty amongst U.S. citizens.

Looking at more recent data from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, it is evident that rates of obesity have only increased, with the states measured as having higher rates of obesity in 2014 staying high in 2019. It is further noticeable that, again consistently with the 2014 census data, states on the west coast tend to have lower rates of obesity, whereas the states with the highest rates of obesity are clustered towards the east of the country. It seems that there has not been a priority in improving the wellbeing of U.S. citizens. This will be a trend to continue watching in the future, particularly for public health authorities.
